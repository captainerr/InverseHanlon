Nobody announces the end of the friend group. There's no meeting, no vote, and no Twitter Spaces postmortem. The group just moves on.

Here are ten telltale signs, ranked roughly by how far gone the friend group is.

## 1. The group chat is now a link dump

The last twelve messages are all reposts with no commentary. Somebody drops a video, one person reacts with an emoji, nobody replies. A group chat that only forwards content is just a feed with extra steps.

## 2. You learn about their life from the timeline

Someone got engaged, someone changed jobs, someone moved cities, and you found out from a tweet with 15 or 16 likes. The group did not know he divorced his ex, let alone that he is dating someone new. We are not sure which half of that is worse.

## 3. Everyone posts about the group instead of to it

Old inside jokes and memes with captions like "miss these days" or "we don't do this anymore." Nostalgia posting is grief with an audience. Nobody sends the meme to the actual people who would get it, because that would require a follow-up.

## 4. The subtweet economy is up and running

Disagreements no longer happen in the chat. They happen in 150 characters aimed vaguely into the void, timed twenty minutes after the chat went quiet.

## 5. Somebody got a following and started talking like a brand account

The friend who used to send you horrific, lewd, and disturbing screenshots now opens a thread with "Let's talk about the why." They have a newsletter. They say "we" about themselves. You are not a friend anymore; you are one of the forgotten day ones, a fan who happens to have their number.

## 6. Nobody likes each other's posts anymore

The mutual likes used to be automatic, a low effort ping that said "I see you." When those stopped, the drift had already happened. Nobody unfollowed. That would require effort, maybe an explanation, perhaps a conviction.

## 7. The meetup is permanently in pre-production

"We should do something" has been said fourteen times across two years. Someone once made a poll. The poll has three votes and no date. The vague plan is a shared reassurance that getting together is theoretically possible.

## 8. Inside jokes got repackaged for strangers

A bit that took four people and one terrible night to build is now a standalone post with context added for the timeline. The joke still works. It belongs to other people now, and the original four are left scratching their heads.

## 9. Strangers in the replies know more than you do

There are people in the mentions who know about the new apartment, the breakup, the health scare. They have opinions. They use the nickname. You have never interacted with them and never will.

## 10. You check on him by scrolling, not texting

Instead of asking how he is, you open his profile, scroll two weeks back, form a rough conclusion, and close the app. You feel updated. Nothing was exchanged. Surveillance is not friendship, no matter how affectionate the motive.

## What to do about it

1. Pick one person from the list.
2. Send an actual message, not a reply to a post, not a reaction to a story.
3. Reference something specific.
4. Propose a real conversation. A bad Tuesday that exists beats a perfect weekend that does not.

Most friend groups don't end from conflict. They end from everyone waiting for someone else to send the first message. Be the one who sends it this week.
