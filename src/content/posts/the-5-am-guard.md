Hi frens,

The alarm goes off before the house does. Before the dog needs out, before the lunches need packing, before the 5 year old wanders in asking where her stuffed bear is. For twenty minutes, it's just me, a cold kitchen, and a decision I have to make fresh every single time: put on the gi, or go back to bed.

**Most mornings I put on the gi.**

I got my blue belt at an age when a lot of guys are hanging up their gis for good, not strapping one on for the first time. I train around a full time job and three kids who do not care, even slightly, that Dad has a tournament in six weeks. Somewhere between the promotion at work, the science fair project, and the 2 AM fever check, jiu jitsu became the one thing in my life that is entirely, selfishly mine (tho I’ve recently got said 5 year old into classes too) and also the thing I have the least legitimate right to be spending time on.

That tension is the whole story. It's also, I've come to believe, the whole point.

## Nobody Warns You About the Math

When you start jiu jitsu in your late 30s, the math is not in your favor and everyone in the room knows it, including you. The purple belts training you are a decade younger with joints that still forgive them. The competition brackets don't care that you haven't slept eight hours in a row since 2021. You roll with guys who have the recovery capacity of a golden retriever and the cardio of someone who isn't also running a household.

Nobody warns you that the hardest opponent isn't the guy across the mat: **it's the calendar.** It's the Tuesday when practice overlaps with your daughter's first dance recital. It's the training camp you can't fully commit to because someone has strep throat. It's the guilt of leaving the house at 6:45 PM, knowing you're trading bedtime stories for bottom side control.

I used to think that guilt was a sign I was doing it wrong. Now I think it's just the toll you pay for wanting two good things at once.

## The Reframe That Actually Changed Things

Here's what shifted for me, and it didn't happen in a single dramatic moment, it happened slowly, over a lot of unremarkable Tuesdays.

I stopped measuring the jiu jitsu against an imaginary version of myself with no wife no kids and no job the guy who trains twice a day and peaks perfectly for the tournament. That guy doesn't exist, and comparing myself to him was just a quiet way of losing.

Instead I started measuring it against *last month's version of me*, the one juggling the exact same constraints. Did I get on the mat more consistently than I did in March? Did I compete even though the prep wasn't perfect? Did I tap less to the same guy I tapped to constantly six months ago? That's the only scoreboard that's actually fair, and it's the only one that keeps you coming back instead of quitting in a fit of self comparison.

Competing itself became less about medals and more about *proof of concept:* proof that discipline built at 5 AM in a cold kitchen actually transfers. It shows up in how I handle a bad week at work. It shows up in how I stay calm when a kid is losing their mind over who gets which stuffed bear right now. Jiu jitsu didn't just give me a new hobby, it gave me a repeatable process for doing hard things on no sleep with imperfect conditions which, as it turns out, describes basically all of parenting and most of adulthood.

## What Actually Made It Possible

A few things, none of them glamorous:

**I stopped trying to find time and started stealing it back.** There was no extra hour hiding somewhere. Every training session came from somewhere else: earlier mornings, fewer scrolling sessions, one less match of a video game I’ve played 10,000 times already.

**I told my family what I was doing and why, out loud.** Not just "Dad has jiu jitsu," but the actual reason: that this was something making me a steadier, more patient version of myself at home. Kids surprise you with how much grace they'll give something once they understand it isn't just Dad disappearing for himself.

**I picked a gym that didn't punish adults with real lives.** Coaches and training partners who understood that "I can only make three classes this week" isn't a lack of commitment, it's a season. That mattered more than any technique I learned.

**I let competing be terrifying and did it anyway.** Standing on a mat in your 40s, gassed, in front of strangers, getting smashed by someone half your age: there's no dignified way to describe how humbling that is. But you find out fast that showing up scared and out of your depth is its own kind of skill, and it's one that pays off everywhere else in life too.

## If You're Standing Where I Was

If you're a parent somewhere in the middle of your life, wondering if it's too late, too selfish, or too impractical to start something hard and compete in it — it isn't. You will not train like you're 22. You will not have the time you wish you had. You will lose matches, sometimes badly, to people who train twice as much as you.

But you will also learn something most people your age never test anymore: **what you're actually capable of when the conditions are far from ideal.** That lesson doesn't stay on the mat. It follows you home, into the job, into the parenting, into every version of showing up that matters.

> Put the gi on. The bed will still be there tomorrow.

-IH
